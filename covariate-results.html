Which contributing factors were correlated with the spread of the virus?

By confronting all the external features related to European regions, we managed to find those that directly influenced the pandemic, and that may cause a higher risk of cases in the future.

Contributing factors analysis


Lombardy among all European regions registered one of the highest number of COVID-19 cases and deaths related to the virus. Therefore, it was natural to wonder if some pre-existing factors may have influenced the spread of the pandemic in this specific area instead of others.

By employing a classification method, we managed to define the level of risk (high or low) for several European regions, as shown in the graph down below. After having classified each region, we needed to understand why a specific region belongs to one of the two risk-class. We used the model random forest to investigate the classification through the contributing factors.

The results of the application of ANOVA methods to the NUTS2 regions, comparing to the levels of the different features, led to some interesting results. For instance, contributing factors related to people proximity (such number of people present in schools and workplaces) are the most impactful for the spread of COVID-19 cases, as predicted by the literature. Since the transmission of the virus occurs mainly through the respiratory route, contacts between people during their daily activities are thus an enhancer for the COVID-19 transmission.



Thousands of hours worked by employees, young people neither in employment nor in education and number of students enrolled are the most significant features. Working activities, schools and little young unemployment are proxies for people's movements and contacts during their daily activities and regions in which people move the most are also the ones with higher density of cases.

On the other hand, life expectancy is also a significant feature for the model. Regions with life expectancy higher than the average show a higher density of cases. As predicted by the literature, symptoms appear more frequently on elderly people that are tested and kept under control, while the majority of young people who contract COVID-19 remain asymptomatic and thus not tested.



The most relevant contributing factors, as shown by the graph, are the Thousand of hours worked, Life Expectancy, followed by the the numbers of people in or out of education, proving that people mobility and interaction with each other are the most impacting features, together with the average age of the population in a specific area.
