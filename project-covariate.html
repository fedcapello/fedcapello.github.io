Contributing factors analysis


The first part of our project concerned the external features that may have influeced the spread of the virus in certain regions instead of others. We followed a common data science workflow, here recapped in 10 crucial steps


1. Understanding the problem


In just a couple of months Covid-19 spread all around the world, infecting and killing people from all geographical areas and social classes, turning into a severe pandemic. In Italy, during 2020, a third of COVID-19 cases and half of the deaths belongs to Lombardy. Why? How did this happen?


2. Exploring the state of the art


The literature review is necessary to answer a few questions to answer. What is COVID-19? Which factors helped the spread of the virus? Are there any existing studies that try to explain why COVID-19 spread in Lombardy and in other specific regions? To help us develop this investigation, One Health Center research groups shared with us their work and their knowledge on this new topic.


3. Defining the aim


The first step to start the analysis is always agreeing on a specific research question. In this project, we aim to discover which external factors could have affected the spread of the virus in Europe and if Lombardy has any particular features compared to othe regions.


4. Evaluating possible solutions


To answer the first research question, we approached the analysis by following a traditional data science workflow. Our aim is to create a model that explains the possible relationships between the external contributing factors of each European region and the number of infections of Covid-19.


5. Looking for contributing factors


Data about contributing external factors are gathered from Eurostat datasets. We created an heterogeneous set of data that we divide in 6 categories of interest: education, population, healthcare, mobility, primary sector and economy. This dataset needs to be analyzed together with the information about Covid-10 cases: data about the infections are retrieved from JRC datasets.


6. Time and space


Data about Covid-19 cases need to be on a regional level and on a daily basis, in order to be analysed, while the the contributing factors should refer to recent year, while maintaining the same granularity as the number of infections. Therefore, we selected information that used NUTS2 standards (regional areas), considering both the first and the second wave of the pandemic.


7. Creating the dataset


All the data are joined together, integrated in one dataset (downloadable here) that contains all the considered European regions, associated to their contributing factors and its Covid cases. The dataset was created after the phases of data exploration, data cleaning and data pre-processing were compleated. Eventually, the final dataset now contins information about 151 European regions, ready to be analysed. that are now ready for the analysis.

8. Modelling the task


After the exploration of the possible solutions, we design a classification task to better suit our objectives. Classification means to predict the class of risk (or severity) associated with the density of Covid-19 cases. We framed 3 possible settings, considering both the first and the second wave:

Binary classification: regions are clustered in low and high severity classes
Multi class classification: regions are clustered in low, medium and high severity classes
Extremes classification: after the creation of 3 classes (low, medium and high), the medium one is removed


9. Showing the results


The binary classifications with information on the second wave proved to be the most reliable classification setting. This model allowed us to interpret what is the absolute importance that each feature of the dataset has in the generation of a prediction: we discovered that work conditions, number of students and life expectancy are the most important features when predicting the severity of the spread of COVID-19.


10. Why Lombardy


Lombardy appears to possess a set of characteristics that define this region as an ideal candidate for the spread of COVID-19. However, it is not the only area that reported these features, as a broader set of regions share similar features, associated with a predisposition to the spread of the virus. Further researches can still be developed.
