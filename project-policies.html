Policies analysis


The second part of our project focused on the policies enacted by governments to limit the spread of the virus. We explored several policies, by describing and comparing the different strategies adopted by the nations, and we managed to obtain a data-driven understanding of their effectiveness. We followed a common data science workflow, here recapped in 9 crucial steps


1. Understanding the problem


In just a couple of months Covid-19 spread all around the world, infecting and killing people from all geographical areas and social classes, turning into a severe pandemic. In Italy, during 2020, a third of COVID-19 cases and half of the deaths belongs to Lombardy. Why? Where our pandemic regulations enough?

2. Exploring the state of the art


The literature review is necessary to answer a few questions to answer. Which non-pharmaceutical interventions were implemented across Europe? Which regulations proved to be the most efficient? To help us evaluating the policies, we took in consideration the existing literature, but since the pandemic is quite recent, not a lot of research has been published regarding this topic. Nonetheless, it helpes us define the most important type of policies to consider in our research.


3. Defining the objective


This research aimed to highlight the main similarities and differences in the application of policies across European regions, both from a geographical and temporal point of view, and to assess their efficacy in limiting the spread of COVID-19.


4. Looking for implemented policies


We collected data about policies regulations from many independent institutions, each with different goals, which are reflected in the type and granularity of information stored. The considered dataset, CoronaNet, is the most comprehensive general-purpose database on government responses in the world.


5. Time and space


In order to be processed and analysed, data about Covid-19 cases and active policies both needed to refer to a regional level and present daily information. Therefore, we selected a dataset that used NUTS2 standards (regional areas), considering both the first and the second wave of the pandemic.

6. Processing the dataset


The original datasets were not adequately organized to allow an efficient analysis, hence we processed them to retain all the relevant information, handle the resolvable inconsistencies or lack of data and reshape them in a more suitable structure. The final dataset (downloadable here) contains information about 11 policy types, their daily activation status in 131 European regions, and the corresponding daily density of Covid-19 cases.

7. Modelling the task


After an initial data exploration, it became evident that non-pharmacologic regulations followed a national rather than regional strategy. Therefore, we aggregated the available information at the national NUTS0 level for the following descriptive analysis. Additionally, we noticed that most policy types have been used much more consistently during the second wave (with several countries applying some of them for the whole period), which made it difficult to effectively discriminate between different intervention approaches; for this reason, in the following we focused on the regulatory response during the first wave.

8. Clustering the countries


We applied a clustering technique to group together countries that adopted similar restriction measures. The obtained results showed a visible subdivision of nations in 3 groups. In particular, it is possible to see that countries which had a softer response to the sanitary emergency (like Poland) and countries which implemented stricter policies for a longer time period (like Italy) are placed in distinct groups.

9. Showing the results


The analysis of the average effect of each policy type on the contagion curve after 7-20 days from their introduction revealed that Restriction and Regulation of Businesses and the Internal Border Restrictions had a peak positive impact on the deceleration of the growth of weekly new positives during the second week (7-13 days), while Lockdown reached its peak effectiveness in the third week (14-20 days); on the other hand, Closure and Regulation of Schools and especially the Quarantine seem to have much lower impact than expected.

Our sources

The data used in our visualizations and analyses is public: all the data were retrieved from Coronanet, a database on government responses to the COVID-19 pandemic.
